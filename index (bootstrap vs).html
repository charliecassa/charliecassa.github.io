9/7/15

and this rain too is an event, literary in this instance, participating
in a long tradition of precipitation—the drop that stains a cigarette
also touched the nose of a mother of the Revolution, missed O’Hara’s
left shirt cuff by inches as he sauntered towards a poetry of vermouth
and gallery light—the rain is of course the formal property of love poems,
which is to say it functions as the primal question: is a lover worth a cold
and what does one do with a lover and a cold in the same room? And if
I am already smoking, and it is raining, is the fact I already have a cold
a limiting case or impediment to love. Choosing to smoke in such conditions
let us, out of idle humanism, consider this a choice, or for the sake of sanity
confusing water and language, believe this: it is raining also in other poems.
